WEBSITE FOR STUDENT ENTREPRENUERSHIP

USER RESEARCH, BRAINSTORMING, PROTOTYPING

I participated in a group project for a class hosted by IDEO and +Acumen, calledHuman-Centered Design for Social Innovation. The course taught me in greater detail the process and methods of human-centered design to create solutions for difficult challenges. I came away with a stronger ability to conduct user research, need-finding, brainstorming, and concept-generation.

Problem

How do we increase social entrepreneurship among young people?

Many students have brilliant ideas, but have no idea where to start. Top-tier universities typically show higher rates of entrepreneurship, where there is an abundance of resources and expertise. Elsewhere, entrepreneurship can seem like something not everyone can do-- especially early on-- and we wanted to help students determine the necessary steps in pursuing a social entrepreneurship goal.

Research

User Research

We conducted many interviews with students and "experts." The "experts" for our purposes was anyone who was currently an entrepreneur or very knowledgable about start-ups. This included asking Dustin Moskovitz several questions during a Q&A session held at UC Berkeley.

Contextual Research

We also decided it would be important to immerse ourselves in the context by visiting a local start-up incubator, called Skydeck. We also interviewed another start-up incubator, called The Foundry@Citrus for a deeper look into the process of transforming an idea into a company.

Analagous Research

This was important in order to see other situations in which goal-making, leadership, and entreprenurship are fostered. This included an entirely student-run volunteer organization called the Suitcase Clinic and local community colleges to see how they are tackling the issue.

Key Findings

We organized our major research findings by theme, in order to find the three most pressing concerns. Our major categories included: experts + mentors, validation, outreach + resources, culture, passion, self awareness + connections, measurable goals + successes. From here, we decided to focus on three themes that we thought were the biggest challenges young people need help with to overcome:

1. Team Building
2. Validation
3. Culture, Outreach, & Resources

Creating "How Might We" Questions in Response to Our Design Challenges

1. How might we match students with complementary skillsets for a team?
2. How might we promote and facilitate quality feedback for students ideas?
3. How might we pair students with mentors that point them in the direction they want?

Ideation


During a brainstorming session, we wrote down every idea we had on post-its that would answer our "How Might We" questions. We then sorted our ideas based on similarity. Ideas included:

  • A mobile-app in which the user swipes yes or no to potential teammates for skill matching
  • Web platform that with a Reddit-like and Piazza-like discussion forum for feedback (scoring based)
  • Searching system for mentors in the area with a regular check-in system
  • Regular online Q&A sessions with entreprenuers
  • Reward and incentive systems for higher-ranked ideas, including premium access

Choosing the Features

We discussed the pros and cons of each idea and chose which one we liked best. Ultimately, we chose to move forward with creating a website with the following features:

  1. Users will create profiles with their skills and be able to search for other people based on desired skills.
  2. Users can post their start-up ideas which users can "upvote," as well as comment on.
  3. Users can request mentorship from "mentors" that are brought onto the site.

Our final concept was to create a webpage that included these major features, allowing minimal risk for students in validating their ideas and finding a mentor that would guide their entrepreneurship goals. For each major interaction, we created a concept page that could be built upon for actual prototyping.
